Notion products are everywhere, which makes this niche easy to dismiss. But the market is not saturated evenly. Broad second-brain dashboards are crowded. Narrow systems tied to a specific business workflow still have room, especially when they are built for micro-agencies and practical solopreneurs rather than productivity hobbyists.

## Step 1 - Estimate the Total Addressable Market (TAM)

The TAM sits inside the productivity software ecosystem and the growing market for creator and business operating systems. That broad market is large, but broad market size is not what makes this niche attractive. What matters is that many small operators want lightweight systems without building them from scratch.

That need continues as more solo businesses try to operate like tiny teams.

## Step 2 - Filter to Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM)

The useful SAM is the segment of buyers who already use Notion or are willing to adopt it for a specific business outcome. This includes solo consultants, content operators, and micro-agencies managing leads, clients, and delivery without heavy software stacks.

The key filter is practicality. If the template feels like a lifestyle dashboard, it becomes optional. If it feels like a business tool, it becomes easier to justify.

## Step 3 - Cross-Platform Research (CPR)

Gumroad is a strong fit because buyers are comfortable purchasing templates and systems there, especially from individual creators. Competition is high in broad Notion categories but much lower in role-specific systems.

That means a generic business dashboard should be treated as saturated. A lead pipeline system for tiny service businesses or a client delivery workspace for micro-agencies can still feel sharp and useful.

## Step 4 - Compute SOM-C

SOM-C improves when the template is built around one workflow with obvious business value. Products that try to do everything inherit more competition and less clarity. Products that solve one workflow well often convert better even with a smaller audience.

In this niche, specificity is the real differentiation multiplier.

## Step 5 - Determine Windows of Opportunity (WOO)

The window remains open where small businesses want more structure but resist complex tools. Many buyers are not looking for a productivity toy. They are looking for a simpler way to handle client work, pipeline tracking, or publishing operations.

Promising directions include:

- client delivery systems for small agencies
- lead tracking dashboards for consultants
- content pipeline workspaces for solopreneurs
- proposal-to-project operating systems for service businesses

## Step 6 - Forecast Market Response (MR)

A new seller should assume some skepticism because buyers have seen many Notion templates already. Month 1 is about proving the template solves a real workflow. Months 2 and 3 can produce first sales if the offer is explained with practical examples and before-and-after clarity. By month 6, sellers with a few connected templates or a template plus guide combination often perform better than one isolated product.

## Step 7 - Strategic Summary

If you want to test Notion on Gumroad, avoid broad productivity templates and focus on a business workflow for a specific kind of operator. The opportunity exists because many buyers still want lightweight business systems without enterprise complexity. The quickest path to first sales is to choose one operational workflow, build a template around it, and market it as a practical business shortcut instead of a general productivity tool.
