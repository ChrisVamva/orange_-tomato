Most bad niche decisions happen before the product is even made. Sellers confuse a visible niche with a profitable one, or they assume that lots of listings automatically means lots of demand. A better approach is to test a niche in layers before investing serious time.

## Step 1 - Estimate the Total Addressable Market (TAM)

Start by asking whether the category belongs to a market that people already spend money in. You do not need a perfect report for every niche, but you do need evidence that the category lives inside a market with real commercial behavior. Adjacent reports, platform behavior, and repeated buyer language can all help establish that.

## Step 2 - Filter to Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM)

Next, narrow the category. Who is the buyer? Which platform are they likely to use? What price range is normal? A niche becomes more useful when you can describe the buyer clearly enough that the product almost writes its own listing.

If you cannot picture the buyer, the niche is probably still too broad.

## Step 3 - Cross-Platform Research (CPR)

Then compare how the niche behaves across platforms. Etsy can be strong for intent-driven digital products. Gumroad can be stronger for expertise-driven products and creator tools. A niche that looks crowded on one platform may still be early on another.

This is where competition should be judged practically. Look for listing quality, repetition, weak positioning, unclear targeting, and signs that buyers still have unanswered needs.

## Step 4 - Compute SOM-C

A profitable niche is not the one with the biggest audience. It is the one where your realistic obtainable share still matters after accounting for competition. That is the value of SOM-C. It forces you to reduce the fantasy market down to the part you can actually reach.

If a niche only works when you assume exceptional performance, it is probably not the right starting niche.

## Step 5 - Determine Windows of Opportunity (WOO)

Good niches usually have a reason they work now. Maybe a buyer segment is underserved. Maybe a new workflow created fresh demand. Maybe the category is growing faster than sellers can package useful products. If you cannot explain the window, the niche may simply be noisy rather than timely.

## Step 6 - Forecast Market Response (MR)

Before building, sketch the likely first-year path. How long will it take to publish? Where will traffic come from? What would count as early traction? A realistic forecast protects you from mistaking slow compounding for failure or early silence for proof that the niche is dead.

## Step 7 - Strategic Summary

Before you build, choose the niche where buyer intent is visible, competition is beatable, and the first version of the product can be shipped quickly. The best opportunities usually appear in narrow categories with a clear buyer situation and an obvious reason the niche matters right now. The quickest path to first sales is to validate the niche in public, package a focused first offer, and support it with content that matches the exact search or problem the buyer already has.
